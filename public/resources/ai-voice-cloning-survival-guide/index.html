Is That Really Your Grandchild?

AI Voice Cloning Survival Guide
Scammers can clone a loved one's voice from just 3–5 seconds of audio. Here's how to protect your family.
"You're not helpless. A code word, a verification call, and 60 seconds of critical thinking can stop a scam cold."
Free Downloadable Resource

Real scenario: A grandmother received a late-night call from someone who sounded exactly like her grandson — panic, details, even his childhood speech pattern. She sent money right away. Her grandson was asleep in his dorm room. The voice was AI.

The New Scam You Need to Know About

AI voice cloning has made it easy for scammers to imitate someone's voice using tiny public clips from social media, voicemail, or online video.

How the Scam Works

Step 1 — Voice Sample

Scammers find short audio from social media, public videos, or old voicemail clips.

Step 2 — Voice Clone

They use AI tools to generate a convincing copy of that voice.

Step 3 — Fake Emergency Call

They call family members pretending to be in trouble: "I'm hurt," "I'm in jail," or "Don't tell anyone."

Step 4 — Urgency + Secrecy

They pressure you to act immediately and isolate you from people who could verify the story.

Step 5 — Payment Demand

They push for immediate money via wire transfer, gift cards, or crypto.

Why This Scam Works

  • You trust your ears: if it sounds like your loved one, your brain believes it.
  • Urgency shuts down critical thinking: panic replaces careful decisions.
  • Secrecy isolates you: "Don't tell anyone" blocks verification.
  • Emotion overrides logic: when someone you love sounds distressed, you act fast.

How to Protect Your Family

1) Set a Family Code Word (Today)

This is one of the strongest defenses. Pick a random phrase only immediate family knows.

  • Share it with immediate family members.
  • If someone calls with an emergency, ask for the code word. No exceptions.

2) Verify Through a Different Channel

  • Hang up.
  • Call back on the saved number you already trust (not the incoming number).
  • Text or call another family member to confirm.

3) Resist Urgency

Scammers use "NOW" pressure. Real emergencies have verifiable channels and do not require secret, instant payment.

4) Limit Public Voice Content

  • Review privacy settings on social platforms.
  • Consider a more generic public voicemail greeting.
  • Be mindful of posting clear, public voice clips.

5) Educate Your Family

Talk with parents, grandparents, and kids so everyone knows this scam exists and knows the code word rule.

What To Do If You Get One of These Calls

  • Stay calm and slow the situation down.
  • Ask specific questions only the real person would know.
  • Use the family code word.
  • Hang up and verify independently.
  • Report it to the FTC at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

If You Already Sent Money

  • Contact your bank or wire service immediately — reversals are sometimes possible.
  • File a police report.
  • Report to FTC and FBI IC3: ReportFraud.ftc.gov and IC3.gov.
  • Do not be embarrassed. These scams fool smart, careful people every day.
