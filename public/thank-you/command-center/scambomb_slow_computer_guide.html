The Universal Troubleshooting Framework
⏱ 10 min read

When your computer acts up, panic is your worst enemy. This framework turns chaos into a checklist. Follow it in order. Do not skip steps.

🔍 Step 1: Check the Obvious First

Before you dive into settings or call for help, verify the basics:

  • POWER IS ON — Is the power light lit? Is the charger plugged in? Is the battery dead?
  • NOT OVERHEATING — Is the fan loud? Is the bottom hot? Move it to a hard, flat surface. Soft beds and couches block air vents.
  • TOO MANY TABS — Count your browser tabs. If you have more than 10, close the ones you are not using. Each tab eats memory.
  • WHEN DID YOU LAST RESTART? — If the answer is "I don't remember," restart now. Sleep mode is not a restart.

🔄 Step 2: The Restart Hierarchy

Restart in this exact order. Stop when the problem goes away.

  1. Restart the app. Close it completely and reopen it. On Windows, right-click the app icon on the taskbar and choose "Close window." On Mac, press Command + Q.
  2. Restart the device. Full power off, wait 10 seconds, power on. Do not just close the lid.
  3. Restart the network. Unplug your router and modem for 2 minutes, then plug them back in. See the Wi-Fi Guide for details.
  4. Call for deeper help. If the first three steps did not fix it, you need a human. See the Trusted Help Directory in the Mega Hub.
💡 The 80/20 Rule

Twenty percent of fixes solve eighty percent of slowdowns. The three fixes above — app restart, device restart, and network restart — solve the vast majority of problems you will ever face.

📝 Step 3: Document Everything

Before you change anything, write it down. This turns panic into data. Your notes will help you, a family member, or a tech support person solve the problem faster.

Write down:

  • What the problem looks like (exact error messages, frozen screen, spinning wheel, etc.)
  • What you were doing when it started
  • Whether it is one program or everything
  • When it started (today, after an update, gradually over weeks)
  • What you have already tried

🧠 Step 4: The "What Changed?" Question

Computers do not break for no reason. Something changed. Ask yourself:

  • Did I install new software?
  • Did I update the operating system?
  • Did I plug in a new device (printer, USB drive, webcam)?
  • Did I change a setting I did not understand?
  • Did someone else use the computer?

The answer to "what changed?" is almost always the cause. Undo the change and see if the problem disappears.

Fixing Slow Performance
⏱ 30 min

A slow computer feels like a broken computer, but it is usually just a cluttered one. These steps clean out the clutter and restore speed without buying new hardware.

🚀 Step 1: Disable Startup Programs

Every program that launches when you turn on the computer steals memory before you even open your first app.

1
Windows

Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc to open Task Manager. Click the "Startup" tab at the top. Look at the "Startup impact" column. Right-click anything rated "High" that you do not need immediately (Spotify, Adobe updater, game launchers) and choose "Disable." Do not disable your antivirus.

2
Mac

System Settings → General → Login Items. Uncheck anything you do not need at startup. Common culprits: Dropbox, Adobe Creative Cloud, Steam, Zoom.

🧹 Step 2: Run Disk Cleanup

Temporary files, old downloads, and system cache pile up over months. Disk Cleanup removes them safely.

1
Windows

Type "Disk Cleanup" in the search bar and open it. Select your main drive (C:). Check every box: Temporary files, Recycle Bin, Thumbnails, Delivery Optimization Files. Click "Clean up system files" and check the boxes again. Click OK. This can free 5–20 GB instantly.

2
Mac

Click the Apple menu → About This Mac → More Info → Storage Settings. Click the "i" next to each category. Review Downloads, Trash, and Large Files. Delete what you do not need.

⚠️ SSD vs. HDD

If your computer has an SSD (solid-state drive, common in computers made after 2015), never defragment it. Defragmentation wears out SSDs. Use "Optimize Drives" instead — Windows does this automatically. If you have an older HDD (hard disk drive), defragmentation can help. Type "Defrag" in the search bar and run it monthly.

🌐 Step 3: Limit Browser Tabs

Every open tab uses memory. Ten tabs can consume more RAM than your entire operating system. Give yourself a five-tab maximum. If you need to save a page for later, use a bookmark or a read-later service like Pocket — do not keep the tab open.

To audit extensions: In Chrome, click the puzzle piece icon → Manage Extensions. Remove anything you do not recognize or use. Extensions are a common source of slowdowns and privacy leaks.

📁 Step 4: Enable Storage Sense (Windows) or Optimize Storage (Mac)

These features automatically clean up old files so you never have to think about it again.

  • Windows: Settings → System → Storage → Storage Sense → Turn ON. Set it to run every month and delete temporary files and Recycle Bin items older than 30 days.
  • Mac: System Settings → General → Storage → Turn ON "Empty Trash automatically" and "Reduce clutter."

🔥 Step 5: Check for Overheating

A hot computer slows itself down on purpose to avoid damage. If your laptop feels like a stovetop:

  • Move it to a hard, flat desk. Beds, couches, and laps block air vents.
  • Blow compressed air into the vents to clear dust. Do this outdoors — dust flies everywhere.
  • Consider a laptop cooling pad ($15–$30). It sits under the laptop and adds extra fans.

Handling Freezes and Crashes
⏱ 15 min

A frozen screen does not mean your computer is broken. It means one app is misbehaving. Learn to kill the app without killing the whole machine.

🛑 Step 1: Force-Close One App

Before you restart the entire computer, try closing just the frozen app. This saves your work in every other program.

1
Windows: Task Manager

Press Ctrl + Shift + Esc (all three at once). This opens Task Manager directly — no need for Ctrl + Alt + Delete. Find the frozen app in the list. It may say "Not Responding." Click it, then click "End task." Wait 10 seconds. If the app closes, save your work in other programs and reopen the frozen app.

2
Mac: Force Quit

Press Command + Option + Esc. A window pops up showing all open apps. Select the frozen one and click "Force Quit." If the entire Mac is frozen and this does not work, hold the power button for 10 seconds to force a shutdown.

💾 Step 2: The Save Habit

The best defense against crashes is not software. It is a finger habit. Press Ctrl + S (Windows) or Command + S (Mac) every few minutes. In Word, Google Docs, and most email programs, this takes half a second and saves hours of reconstruction.

💡 Make It Automatic

Most modern programs auto-save. In Microsoft Word, go to File → Options → Save → check "AutoSave OneDrive and SharePoint Online files by default on Word." In Google Docs, auto-save is always on. But do not rely on it alone. Ctrl + S is your insurance policy.

🔄 Step 3: When the Whole Machine Freezes

If the mouse does not move, the keyboard does nothing, and Task Manager will not open, the entire operating system is stuck.

  1. Wait 60 seconds. Sometimes the computer is just thinking hard. Give it a full minute before you panic.
  2. Try Ctrl + Alt + Delete. On Windows, this sometimes breaks through a freeze and shows a blue screen with options. Choose "Task Manager."
  3. Hard power off. Hold the power button for 10 seconds. The screen goes black. Wait 10 more seconds. Press the power button once to restart. You will lose unsaved work, but the hardware is unharmed.

🧊 Step 4: Preventing Future Freezes

Freezes usually happen for one of three reasons:

  • Memory is full. Close unused programs and browser tabs. Consider upgrading RAM if you have less than 8 GB.
  • One app is buggy. If the same app freezes repeatedly, uninstall it and reinstall the latest version from the official website.
  • The computer is overheating. See the Slow Performance section for cooling tips.

When Slowness Means Malware
⏱ 1-2 hours

Sometimes a slow computer is not cluttered — it is infected. Malware (viruses, spyware, ransomware) can hide for weeks before you notice. Know the warning signs and act fast.

🚨 The Warning Signs

One symptom alone does not mean infection. But if you see three or more of these together, assume the worst and act immediately:

  • SUDDEN SLOWDOWN — Computer was fine yesterday, unusable today
  • POP-UPS EVERYWHERE — Ads appear even when the browser is closed
  • HOMEPAGE CHANGED — Your usual search engine is now something you have never heard of
  • NEW TOOLBARS — Extra buttons appeared in your browser that you did not install
  • UNKNOWN PROGRAMS — Apps in your Start menu or Applications folder you do not recognize
  • BATTERY DRAINS FAST — Malware runs in the background, consuming power
  • FRIENDS GET WEIRD EMAILS — Your email account is sending spam without your knowledge

🛡️ Step 1: Run Windows Defender (Windows) or XProtect (Mac)

Every modern computer comes with built-in antivirus. Use it first.

1
Windows Defender

Type "Windows Security" in the search bar and open it. Click "Virus & threat protection" → "Quick scan." If it finds threats, let it remove them. Then run a "Full scan" — this takes 1-2 hours but checks every file. Leave the computer plugged in and do not use it during the scan.

2
Mac XProtect

Macs do not have a manual scan button, but XProtect runs automatically in the background. To force a check, restart your Mac while holding the Shift key (Safe Mode). The system runs a basic diagnostic during startup. Restart normally afterward.

🔍 Step 2: Run Malwarebytes for a Second Opinion

No single antivirus catches everything. Malwarebytes is the gold standard for a second scan. It is free for manual scans.

  1. Go to malwarebytes.com on your phone (not the infected computer, in case the browser is hijacked).
  2. Download the free version.
  3. Install it on the infected computer.
  4. Run a "Threat Scan." This takes 15-30 minutes.
  5. If it finds threats, click "Quarantine" and then "Restart" when prompted.
⚠️ The Two-Scan Rule

Always run two different scanners. Windows Defender catches about 95% of threats. Malwarebytes catches a different 95%. Running both gives you 99.75% coverage. Never rely on just one.

🔄 Step 3: Reset Your Browser

Malware often hijacks your browser settings — homepage, search engine, and extensions. Even after removing the malware, the browser may still be compromised.

  • Chrome: Settings → Reset settings → Restore settings to their original defaults.
  • Edge: Settings → Reset settings → Restore settings to their default values.
  • Safari: Safari menu → Settings → Privacy → Manage Website Data → Remove All. Then Settings → Extensions → uninstall anything you do not recognize.

🆘 Step 4: When to Escalate Immediately

Some infections are beyond home repair. Call a professional if you see any of these:

  • Ransomware message: A screen says your files are encrypted and demands payment in Bitcoin. Do not pay. Turn off the computer immediately and call a data recovery specialist.
  • Banking login stolen: You see transactions you did not make. Call your bank's fraud line immediately — before you do anything else.
  • Identity theft signs: New credit cards arrive in the mail that you did not apply for. File a police report and contact the three credit bureaus to freeze your credit.
💡 Prevention Is the Best Cure

After you clean the infection, prevent the next one: keep your operating system updated, use a password manager so you do not reuse passwords, enable MFA on every account, and never click links in unexpected emails or texts. See the Password Playbook and Foundation Guide for details.
