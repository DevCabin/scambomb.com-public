RECRUITER IMPERSONATION CASE STUDY

This case study explains a common job scam pattern: fake recruiter outreach, fast pressure to trust the message, unrealistic pay promises, and a fake-check setup for equipment or supplies.

What happened

  • The first message looked like a normal recruiter follow-up.
  • The so-called interview was kept entirely in text messages.
  • The promised pay was unusually high for simple remote work.
  • The scam then shifted to “we’ll send a check for equipment.”

Red flags

Unexpected recruiter outreach

A surprise message from a recruiter can be fake, especially if you did not apply.

Text-only interview

Real employers usually use official email, phone calls, or video meetings.

Pay that sounds too good

Very high pay for simple remote tasks is a common scam lure.

Check for supplies

This is a classic fake-check scam warning sign.

How the scam can escalate

  • First step: The scammer gets attention and tries to build trust quickly.
  • Next step: They may ask for personal details, banking information, or other sensitive information.
  • Main financial risk: The fake check can lead to direct money loss.
  • Longer-term risk: Shared personal information can lead to identity theft or other follow-up scams.

What to do now

  • Stop contact and save screenshots, messages, email addresses, phone numbers, and any documents.
  • If money was sent or a check was deposited, call the bank or payment provider immediately.
  • If personal information was shared, treat it as a possible identity theft risk and begin securing accounts.
  • Report the scam to the FTC, IC3, the job platform used, and the real company being impersonated.
